INT. YOUR IMAGINATION — DAY  ·  What Is Story?

Story is how we make sense of the world.

What triggers the pleasure we feel when a story hooks us? Not lyrical language or vivid images — Curiosity is the trigger. The desire to find out what happens next. It is the STORY that captivates the brain.

Without knowing what a story is and how it works, you won't be able to write a screenplay. Many people think they have a great idea for a movie — but most of the time the idea doesn't include a real story at all. It's merely a description of what happened.

From the very first sentence, a story must revolve around how someone solves an unexpected problem they simply can't avoid. The reader needs to sense this immediately. Nothing grabs the brain quicker than a surprise.

Three things every reader's brain hunts for instantly:

→  Whose story is this?

→  What's happening here?

→  What is at stake?

Based on Lisa Cron's teaching.

Three Types of Screenplay

Spec Script — Written speculatively, in the hope of being purchased by a producer.

Commissioned Script — Written by hired professionals for a specific project or franchise.

Shooting Script — Altered for production: scenes numbered, pages locked.

INT. THE WRITER'S ROOM — CONTINUOUS  ·  The Craft

Rules to follow — or break.

Craft gives you the questions you need to answer. It maximizes your talent and radically reduces your rewriting time. When you know the rules, you break them knowingly — and that makes your screenplay original.

When you break them unknowingly, your screenplay becomes aimless and out of balance. You don't have to follow every rule — you just have to know them. Learn the craft. Then practice it: write.

INT. THE WRITER'S DESK — NIGHT  ·  Principles & Commandments

Rules to live by as a screenwriter.

The principles and commandments below are the distilled wisdom of working screenwriters who learned these truths the hard way — through drafts, rejections, and rewrites. Study them. Internalize them. Then decide when to follow them and when to break them — but always knowingly.

by Daniel Martin Eckhart

  • 01
    Write daily.You need a strong writing muscle to succeed — so exercise it.
  • 02
    Enjoy procrastination.Procrastination is life — never feel guilty about it.
  • 03
    Trust your instincts.Learn from others but never depend on them.
  • 04
    Believe in yourself.If you don't, no one ever will.
  • 05
    Suck it up.Learn from rejection — it'll make you stronger.
  • 06
    Know your world.Make movie history, films and scripts part of your essence.
  • 07
    Network.You'll never have a career if you don't get out there.
  • 08
    Love what you do.If writing doesn't make you happy, stop.
  • 09
    Be generous.Spread your ideas — don't hide them.
  • 10
    Be a craftsman/woman.It may become art in time.
  • 11
    Deliver on time.Never miss a deadline — not even one you gave yourself.
  • 12
    Collaborate.You'll never make a film happen on your own.

by Robert McKee

  • I
    Thou shalt not take the crisis out of the protagonist's hands.The anti-deus ex machina commandment.
  • II
    Thou shalt not make life easy for the protagonist.Nothing progresses in a story except through conflict.
  • III
    Thou shalt not give exposition for exposition's sake.Dramatize it. Convert exposition to ammunition.
  • IV
    Thou shalt not use false mystery or cheap surprise.
  • V
    Thou shalt respect your audience.The anti-hack commandment.
  • VI
    Thou shalt know your world as God knows this one.The pro-research commandment.
  • VII
    Thou shalt not complicate when complexity is better.Use all three levels: Intra-personal, Inter-personal, Extra-personal.
  • VIII
    Thou shalt seek the end of the line.Take characters to the farthest reaches of conflict within the story's realm of probability.
  • IX
    Thou shalt not write on the nose.Put a subtext under every text.
  • X
    Thou shalt rewrite.
